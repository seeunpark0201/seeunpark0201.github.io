Die Trying Collective (DTC):
Building sustainable habits

Barbarian Summer Internship 2019

Overview

DTC is a product that promotes employees to form a healthy habit around sustainability by being conscious of plastic usage in the office. Users can see a relatable impact that they make by using less plastic, and can also see how they’re doing compared to their co-workers.

Role

I was involved in doing research, initiating a new design direction for the product, identifying problem areas, conducting usability tests, and prototyping

Research


I joined this project after the primary research and initial wireframes had already been done. The premise of this project was to promote a sustainable lifestyle within the office by motivating the employees to use metal utensils instead of plastic utensils. There would be a tablet placed in the office kitchen, and every time an employee uses a metal utensil, they would tap on the screen. The image below shows initial screens that had been made before I joined.

Problem - pt.1


People are not interested in the product because they don’t know the significance of the interaction.


To figure out how the people around the office would be interested in interacting with the product, I conducted semi-structured interviews with some of the employees and noticed that most people said they would be interested, as long as the app itself had value and didn’t take too much of their time.


Ideation


  1. How might we initially attract people to use our product?

  2. How might we incentivize people to choose an action that benefits the environment over the convenience of plastic products?

I came up with including the following behaviors on the app to capture the oppotunity space that I’ve identified:

  • Rewarding good behavior (social recognition, showing direct impact)
  • Emphasizing consequences of plastic usage

Rewarding good behavior will help users initially be attracted to the product, and emphasizing consequences of plastic usage will help users understand the significance of their action, thus leading the users to be conscious of the impact their actions have and ultimately form a habit.

Features that we deemed appropriate as “rewards” for this product were:

  • Connecting the numbers to digestable and relatable facts (i.e. recycling one glass bottle can power a computer for 30 minutes).
  • A fun microinteraction of ‘flinging’ the utensils instead of just a tap
  • A big moment of acknowledgement for reaching a certain number

Usability Testing


Insights:


  1. People want to know more global impacts that they’re making by reducing plastic waste.

  2. Interested in personalized data reports - “How much impact did I make the past month?

  3. People want some competition.

We conducted usability tests on 9 employees on the prototype above. With the help of my manager, I wrote a script that focused on finding out improvements to make:

  • How well do the users interact with the app in the way it was designed to?
  • How do users react to the reward?
  • How interesting is the product - does it succeed in making users wanting to find out more?

Problem - pt.2


In doing the research and ideating, a question popped into my head:

How can we make the app ubiquitous such that users can keep track of the impact their actions have in their everyday lives - outside of the office?


I realized that this product would have so much more potential if it lived as a mobile app, as phones have become such an integral part of our lives. After proposing the idea to my manager, we agreed to think about an ecosystem for this product to live outside of the office kichen.


Competitive Analysis

In order to better understand what kind of products exist that keep track of a certain action, I found and analyzed 6 different apps - 3 of them were calorie counters, and the other 3 counted carbon footprints. The main takeaways were:

  1. The act of logging a certain action helps people be more conscious of that behavior and in turn empower them to control it.

  2. Currently there are no apps that track plastic usage and convert it to a relatable fact easily understood by users.

From all of the research findings, I identified two specific problem spaces:

  1. How might we motivate users to continue the act of reducing plastic waste in their everyday lives with our product?

  2. How might we help users easily understand the impact they are making?
